Overview of PIE grammar

English often uses:

  • word order:
    • The boy hugged the tree.
    • The tree hugged the boy.

to indicate the role of a nominal in a sentence

Word order in English tells us the subject vs the object

The boy hugged the tree. - ‘The boy’ is the subject of the sentence. - ‘The tree’ is the object of the sentence.

The subject of a sentence tells us who or what is verbing. The direct object of a sentence tells us who or what is verbed.

Word order in English tells us the role of the nominals in a sentence

-   The tree hugged the boy.

What is the subject?  What is the object?

Languages with case systems use case markers to indicate the role of the nominals:

  • Latin:
    • Agricola poetam videt. ‘The farmer sees the poet’
    • Agricolam poeta videt. ‘The poet sees the farmer’
    • Poeta agricolam videt. ‘The poet sees the farmer’
    • Poetam agricola videt. ‘The farmer sees the poet’

Word order is more flexible because the words themselves contain the information about the roles in the sentence.

In the sentences above, which ending indicates the nominative case (the subject) and which one is the accusative case (the direct object)?

When translating:
Find the ending → identify case/number/gender → identify word’s function in the sentence.

PIE Cases

  • Nominative: subject (“the wolf”)
  • Vocative: address (“yo wolf!”)
  • Accusative: direct object / motion-goal (“see the wolf”)
  • Instrumental: means (“with a wolf”)
  • Dative: recipient / “to/for the wolf”
  • Ablative: source (“from the wolf”)
  • Genitive: possession / “of” (“tail of the wolf”)
  • Locative: location (“in/on/at the wolf”)

English verbs also use:

  • endings
    • The boy hugs the tree.
    • The boy hugged the tree.
  • helper words
    • The boy could hug the tree.
    • The boy can hug the tree.
    • The boy will hug the tree.
  • helper words and endings
    • The boy is hugging the tree.
    • The boy has hugged the tree.

PIE verbs often use:

  • endings

    • verbs: person/number/tense/aspect/mood

  • stem alternations (ablaut)

  • derivational morphology (suffixes)

The basic units

  • Root: core meaning (e.g. *bʰer- ‘to carry’)
  • Stem: root + suffix + ablaut grade (e.g. *bʰére- ‘carry’, present stem)
  • Ending: tells you the grammatical job (e.g. *-ti, 3rd singular)

The basic units

Put it together:

bʰéreti ‘he/she/it carries’

The basic units

You can change the ending:

bʰéresi ‘you carry’

The basic units

You can change the stem:

bʰoréyeti ‘(s)he makes (someone) carry’

The basic units

Or the root:

léĝeti ‘(s)he gathers’

Verbs: what you track

  • Person/number: 1/2/3; sg/du/pl
  • Tense/aspect: present, aorist, perfect
  • Mood: indicative, imperative, optative, subjunctive
  • Voice: active, mediopassive

Verbs: what you track

  • Person/number
  • Tense/aspect
  • Mood
  • Voice

When translating:
Figure out who’s doing the action from the verb’s ending first. From there, figure out the tense, etc.

PIE Word Order

  • Often characterize as SOV (subject + object + verb), but it’s very flexible.
    • Endings carry a lot of the load, so word order can be freer.

Learning How to Translate

Translating PIE

H₂aláh₂. h₁nómn̥ moy H₁inigṓ montóyah₂ h₁esti. Gʷʰégʷʰens pəh₂térm̥ méne. Pékʷeswe mr̥téyey.

How on earth can be begin to translate this?

Steps to Translate

  1. identify verbs
  2. identify subjects/objects via case
  3. find other nominals and words to complete your translation
  4. shuffle word order to make it more English-like
